# The Poet

![Sri Prabhudatta Shastri, 1892–1979](../../../images/prabhudatta_shastri.png)

Among the great Mahakavyas to emerge in modern Sanskrit literature, 'Ganapati Sambhavam' holds a place of honor. This wondrous work of ten sargas, composed in the spirit of the ancient Mahakavi Kalidasa's 'Kumarasambhavam', was written by that crest-jewel among poets — Mahamahopadhyaya, Vidya-Vachaspati Pandit Prabhudatta Shastri.

Pandit Prabhudatta Shastri was born into the illustrious Mishra lineage, in a village called 'Tatarpur' west of the town of Alwar in the state of Rajasthan. His great-grandfather was Pandit Shaligrama Mishra, a great scholar who wrote commentaries on the Srimad Bhagavatam and other texts, and who copied out the Valmiki Ramayana with his own hand.

His grandfather was Sri Ramapratapa Mishra, a great benefactor who offered Ayurvedic medical care to the people free of charge, without expecting any fee; the local people, pleased with his service, lovingly called him 'Lattu Mishra'. His father was Sri Ramasharana Mishra. Shastri-ji was born in the Vikrama year 1946 (1892 CE), on Phalguna Shukla Panchami, a Thursday. When he was but two years old, his father passed into the eternal abode, and he was raised in the care of his compassionate mother and his grandfather.

For his education, he was sent to a town called 'Mandawa' in the Marwar region. There he studied all the shastras under teachers named Pandit Vilasaram Shukla, Mohan Lal, and Suryananda. He later moved to the city of Delhi, where he taught as a schoolteacher and earned great fame and renown.

His profound scholarship won him many prestigious honors. At the special Gita-artha competitions held at Kurukshetra, under the patronage of the Prime Minister of Patiala, Dyaliram, he secured first place — in resolving apparent contradictions among commentaries on the Gita — and won the 'Gold Medal'. At the All-India Sanskrit Literary Conference held in Patna, he composed a marvelous *samasya-purti* (extemporaneous completion of a verse) on the word 'Sanskritam' and received the title 'Sarvottama Kavi' (Best of Poets).

Alongside this Mahakavya 'Ganapati Sambhavam', Pandit Prabhudatta Shastri composed many other rare works in the Sanskrit language. A list of those works:

- Srimad Bhagavad Gita Vyangya Mandakini (a commentary)
- Virata Rupa Darshanam
- Dhanvantari Janmamritam
- Gandhi Nandi Shraddhamritam and Charakha Vandanamritam
- Samskrita Vagvijayamu (a play)
- Samskrita Vak Saundaryamritam
- Rashtradhwajamritam
- Jhansiswari Shauryamritam
- Mahimna Stotram (with a Hindi commentary)
- Mahalakshmi Puja

Without abandoning the conventions of classical poetic composition, Pandit Prabhudatta Shastri was a rare and innovative craftsman who wove modern nationalist sentiments, the spirit of India's freedom struggle, the valor of Jhansi Lakshmibai, Mahatma Gandhi's charkha, and the national flag into Sanskrit verse. His compositions, appearing as they do in the modern age, are priceless ornaments bestowed upon the Sanskrit language.
