# Ganapati Sambhavam — An Introduction to This Wondrous Mahakavya

We are all well acquainted with the Mahakavya 'Kumarasambhavam', composed by the great poet Kalidasa. If the younger brother Kumaraswami has so great an epic devoted to him, how fitting would it be if the elder brother Ganapati — foremost among those worthy of worship — also had a Mahakavya of his own? That very wondrous thought was the seed from which Mahamahopadhyaya Pandit Prabhudatta Shastri's Mahakavya 'Ganapati Sambhavam' arose! This work of ten sargas is not confined merely to the flavor of devotion (Bhakti-rasa); it weaves in modern social ideas as well, offering readers a fresh and invigorating experience.

This poetic journey begins delightfully with a description of the natural beauty of the snow-capped Himalayas, Kashmir, and Nepal. The wedding of Shiva and Parvati, and thereafter Parvati Devi breathing life into a clay figure through her yogic power to create Ganapati, are described most wondrously. When the boy, standing guard at his mother's command, obstructs none other than Shiva himself as he returns home — the ensuing logical debate (Shastrartha) between the two over the question "Who is a father?" holds the reader spellbound.

Thereafter, the humorous war of words between Ganapati and Parashurama, in which he loses one tusk and becomes 'Ekadanta' (the one-tusked), unfolds with great zest. Likewise, Ganapati's swift writing of the Mahabharata alongside Veda Vyasa, and his winning — through sheer cleverness — of the divine 'modaka' (sweet dumpling) offered by the gods, by circling his parents in competition with Kumaraswami, bring forth much laughter.

The most fascinating episode in this Mahakavya is its comparison of Ganapati's very form to the modern ideal of 'Democracy'! The poet explains how a leader with large ears and small eyes ought to be — one who listens closely to the people's words while observing with subtle discernment. That the poet should apply modern national ideals — equal rights for all people, equality between man and woman, the eradication of poverty, and religious harmony — to the very form of Ganapati is testament to his boundless intellect.

While relishing the style of classical poetry, this is a wondrous Mahakavya that everyone wishing to witness a blend of devotion, logic, humor, nationalism, and modern political thought must read without fail!
