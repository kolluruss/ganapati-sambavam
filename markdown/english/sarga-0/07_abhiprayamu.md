## Opinion of Archana Prakashan publishers, Nagpur

This is a matter of the highest fortune for the world of Sanskrit, for scholars capable of composing such Mahakavyas still exist even in this present age. Mahakavyas of this kind were once composed even before the invasions of foreign powers. Perhaps no one has come across another Mahakavya written in the tradition of Kalidasa, the preceptor among poets, and his '**Kumara Sambhavam**'. Indeed, it was that very Mahakavya, 'Kumara Sambhava', that kindled the desire to compose this 'Ganapati Sambhava'. A younger brother such as Kumaraswami could only rejoice at receiving a Kavya that glorifies the fame of his elder brother, could he not? Thus, even greater foresight was shown in the choice of subject for this Kavya than in its actual composition.

The beauty of this Kavya is increased fourfold by the very names given to its sargas (cantos). The other matters contained within it will become known simply by reading the table of contents; nevertheless, we offer you here some indication of what captivated us and led us to undertake its publication:

(1) **'Himagiri Parichayamu' (Introduction to the Himalayas)** — Here, the description of Kashmir, the very chest of today's Himalayas, and of Nepal, its head, is well worth beholding. The parallel drawn between Kashmir and the Indian national flag, and the very form of the Indian soldiers who stand guard upon the Himalayas, are memorable indeed. The advertisement, as it were, of their saffron and musk, is composed with great wit, without need of any agent. The blessed sight of Amarnath and Pashupatinath is made available here, right at home.

(2) **'Girisha Girija Panigrahanamu' (The Wedding of Shiva and Parvati)** — Here, the splendor of marriage in the Indian tradition, the names of Shiva's father, grandfather, and great-grandfather, and the farewell (vidaayi) of Parvati Devi, are most joyous to behold.

(3) **'Yogashakti Chamatkriti' (The Wonder of Yogic Power)** — This shows both Shiva's yogic worship and the manner in which Parvati Devi's yogic power bore fruit upon the son she made of clay.

(4) **'Shastraartha Shastribeevamu' (The Debate, and Its Turn to Arms)** — The debate (Shastrartha) that Shiva conducts with his own son on the question of fatherhood, and its astonishing transformation thereafter into a matter of arms, is a marvel of craft.

(5) **'Gajamanujayojanamu' (The Union of Elephant and Man)** — Here lies the secret of how an elephant and a human being come to stand joined as one, along with the praise of the scholars of Kashi.

(6) **Tanaikadanta Prasangah (The Episode of the Single Tusk)** — The story of how Ganesha's tusk was broken by Parashurama is most instructive.

(7) **'Mahabharata Lekhakhyanamu' (The Tale of the Writing of the Mahabharata)** — We have all heard of this before, but the manner in which that writing actually took place may be seen here for the very first time.

(8) **'Devamodakopaharamu' (The Divine Offering of the Modaka)** — The tales of how the modaka (sweet dumpling) came to be connected with Ganesha are sweeter still than the modaka itself.

(9) **'Ganashasanamu' (The Rule of the Ganas)** — The thorough exposition of its characteristics, its methods, and the means to its stability is well worth the attention of today's masters of statecraft.

(10) **'Kavyantara Pushparpanamu' (An Offering of Flowers from His Other Works)** — Here the poet has given, in a most delightful manner, samples of his other compositions along with an account of his own life.

Other matters — such as the birth of a daughter to the Himalaya mountain, the images of reconciliation set up on Kailasa and other such virtuous themes, the simplicity of meditative samadhi, and passages on devotion to the motherland that draw one toward the many unattained benefits of education — are exceedingly delightful.

It is a widely held view that the publication of Sanskrit Mahakavyas is not an encouraging prospect from a financial standpoint. For this very reason, this Mahakavya had not come to light for many years. It is only in the hope that the scholarly community of India will surely embrace such a work that this venture in publishing has been undertaken. Had it remained unpublished, it stood in danger of being lost to time altogether — and the loss of such a Kavya would have been a matter of grief to us all.

We do not merely hope that this endeavor of ours will succeed — we hold full confidence in it, and confidence too that we shall bring to light still more unpublished works belonging to the Sanskrit language.

* The Publishers.
