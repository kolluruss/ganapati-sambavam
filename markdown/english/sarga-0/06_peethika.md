# **A Brief Preface**

Kavya-shastra (the science of poetics) is the third, blissful form among the sat-chit-ananda aspects of the Supreme Brahman. It is for this very reason that scholars call it Shabda-Brahman (the Word as Brahman). The saying **"kāvya śāstra vinodena kālo gacchati dhīmatām"** ("the time of the wise passes in the delight of poetics and letters") testifies to the irresistible attraction this subject holds.

In the case of Artha-Brahman (Brahman as meaning/substance), illustrations such as the rope mistaken for a snake seem to suggest its transience and its unfitness for worship. But Shabda-Brahman is ever fit for worship, and so even yogis adore it alongside words such as '**tat sat**' and '**tattvamasi**'. Hence it is established that Kavya-shastra and Shabda-Brahman are one and the same, merely synonyms.

The principal etymology of the word 'Kavya' is this: it derives from '**kena**' (by Brahman) and '**āvyam**' (that which is to be protected, attained, or illumined) — that is, Kavya; here the root 'ava' carries the senses of motion, radiance, and protection. Or, it may mean that which is to be worn 'ke' (upon the head). Or, it may mean that the poet (kavi) is one born 'kavau' (in the Supreme Brahman), for indeed the Shrutis give the Supreme Self the name '**kaviḥ svayambhūḥ paribhūḥ**'. Or, a kavi is one who weaves cloth in water (ke). Or again, following the root '**kavṛ varṇane**' (to describe), a kavi is one who describes even an ordinary object in an extraordinary way, or one who is a seer of what is beyond the ordinary (krāntadarśī).

Since it is composed by a kavi (poet), it is the Kavya of the kavi. Or, 'kavyam' refers to the food and other offerings given with reverence to the departed ancestors dwelling in heaven; in the same way, that which is offered with reverence to the ancient great poets such as Sri Kalidasa is also called Kavya. Thus this Shabda-Brahman, called by the many-meaning name Kavya, is of two kinds. The first — taking refuge in the mouths of the great sages, assuming written form, and remaining fixed permanently in one place like the water of wells, springs, and ponds — accomplishes all the world's affairs through names such as the Vedas. The second — arising in the mind of the one in the form of a poet, like '**Shatarupa**' (she of a hundred forms), truly assuming hundreds of forms, and ever shining like the sun and moon — is that renowned by the name '**Kavya**'. Just as the son of Shiva the Conqueror of Death (Kumaraswami), wishing to conquer the six directions, united his six faces as if into a single lotus, so too the wise well know that this Kavya proceeds with an eye toward six purposes.

**"kāvyaṃ yaśase'rthakṛte vyavahāravide śivetarakṣataye |**
**sadyaḥ paranirvṛtaye kāntāsammitatayopadeśayuje"** —

(Kavya bestows fame, wealth, and knowledge of worldly affairs; it destroys inauspiciousness; it grants immediate bliss of Brahman; and it instructs pleasantly, as a beloved wife does). It is precisely because of these purposes that scholars take such interest in the composition of Kavya. As the saying goes, "even a fool does not undertake any task without some purpose in mind" — how much greater, then, is the delight of the discerning in a task that yields two fruits at once. We hear it said in the world that "a certain sage, holding his water-pot and darbha grass, pours water at the foot of a mango tree; by that water the mango tree is nourished, and the ancestors are satisfied — thus a single act accomplishes two purposes." But this thing called Kavya is soft as fresh butter, and bestows many fruits at once. It is for this very reason that great poets have fashioned many a Kavya-body, free from the fears of old age and death. Among these, the Mahakavya "Kumarasambhavam," composed by Sri Kalidasa, telling the story of Kumaraswami, the son of the Lord, has long adorned the faces of the learned as an ornament.

As I write these very lines, a joyous wish, too, arose fortunately in my own heart — namely, that if scholars take such delight in the Kavya of the younger son, Kumaraswami, then surely a Kavya composed upon the elder Lord Ganapati would without fail captivate the minds of the learned. Moreover, while people know Kumaraswami only a little, Ganapati is renowned to one and all, from the smallest child to the eldest elder. Even a boy ploughing a field drives his plough while uttering Ganapati's name; and he holds a place among the five principal deities. By the very custom of saying "śrī gaṇeśaḥ kṛtaḥ" (the auspicious mark of Ganesha has been made) at the outset of every undertaking, his very name shines as synonymous with beginnings. Having considered all this, I set out, to the best of my humble ability, to worship that Lord in this way. I therefore earnestly and humbly beg you to examine this entire work and, by granting your consent, bring this effort of mine to fruition.

If any faults natural to human frailty should appear anywhere in this work, I fold my lotus-like hands and beg that you kindly bring them to my notice. And should you, engaged in boundless undertakings, be unable to examine this entire work, I firmly believe that you will at least examine it after the manner of '**shalākā-parīkṣā**' (piercing a book with a needle to sample a page) or '**sthālīpulāka-nyāya**' (judging whether all the rice is cooked by testing a single grain). Whichever great souls read this work and, with a gracious heart, bestow their blessings upon it, I close by expressing my gratitude for their causeless compassion, acknowledging my great debt to them.

**"āparitoṣād vidhuṣāṃ na sādhu manye prayoga vijñānam"** (I do not consider my endeavor a success until the learned themselves are fully satisfied).

— Prabhudatta Shastri
